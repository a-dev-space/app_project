2. Basic Number Properties

  • Additive identity: $a+0=a$ ‚Äì zero changes nothing when added.
  • Multiplicative identity: $a√ó1=a$ ‚Äì one changes nothing when multiplied.
  • Additive inverse (opposite): $a+(-a)=0$.

3. Absolute Value and Opposites

The absolute value of a number is its distance from 0 on the number line: $\;|{-4}|=4,\;|7|=7,\;|{-3-4}|=7.$ It is always non-negative.

Two numbers that are the same distance from 0 but on opposite sides are opposites: 4 and ‚àí4. Note that 0 is its own opposite.

4. Signed Number Operations and Rules

When working with positives and negatives:

  • Addition/Subtraction: Same sign ‚Üí add magnitudes, keep sign; different sign ‚Üí subtract and keep sign of larger magnitude. Example: $(-7)+5=-2$.
  • Multiplication/Division: Same sign ‚Üí positive; different sign ‚Üí negative. Even number of negatives ‚Üí +, odd ‚Üí ‚àí.
  • Zero rules: Any number times 0 is 0; division by 0 is undefined; $0/a=0$ for $a‚â†0$.

5. Order of Operations (PEMDAS)

To evaluate any expression:

  1. Parentheses and grouping symbols first (innermost to outermost).
  2. Exponents and roots.
  3. Multiplication and division (left to right).
  4. Addition and subtraction (left to right).

Example: $8-3√ó(2+1)^2=8-3√ó9=8-27=-19.$

6. Divisibility and Prime Concepts

A number $a$ is divisible by $b$ if $a√∑b$ has no remainder. Short tests help identify factors:

  • 2 ‚Üí last digit even (0,2,4,6,8)
  • 3 ‚Üí sum of digits divisible by 3
  • 4 ‚Üí last two digits form number divisible by 4
  • 5 ‚Üí last digit 0 or 5
  • 6 ‚Üí divisible by both 2 and 3
  • 9 ‚Üí digit sum divisible by 9
  • 10 ‚Üí last digit 0

Prime number: has exactly two factors (1 and itself). Examples: 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13. Composite: has more than two factors (e.g. 6 = 2√ó3).

Every integer > 1 can be expressed uniquely as a product of prime factors (prime factorization), such as $84=2^2√ó3√ó7$.

7. Greatest Common Factor (GCF) and Least Common Multiple (LCM)

  • GCF: The largest integer that divides each of two numbers exactly. Find by listing factors or using prime factorization (min exponents). Example: GCF(18, 24)=6.
  • LCM: The smallest positive integer divisible by each number (max exponents). Example: LCM(18, 24)=72.
  • Relation: $\text{GCF}(a,b)√ó\text{LCM}(a,b)=a√ób$.

Fractions and Decimals

1. Meaning of a Fraction

A fraction represents part of a whole: the numerator counts how many parts are taken, and the denominator tells how many equal parts the whole is divided into. Example: $\frac34$ means ‚Äúthree parts out of four equal parts of the whole‚Äù.

2. Equivalent Fractions

3. Simplifying Fractions

  • Find the greatest common factor (GCF) of numerator and denominator.
  • Divide both by that GCF.

Example: $\frac{630}{945}=\frac{630√∑315}{945√∑315}=\frac23.$

4. Improper Fractions and Mixed Numbers

  • Improper fraction: numerator ‚â• denominator, e.g. $\frac{7}{4}$.
  • Mixed number: combination of whole + proper fraction, e.g. $1\frac34$.
  • Convert: $$ 1\frac34=\frac{7}{4},\qquad \frac{7}{4}=1\frac34. $$

5. Adding and Subtracting Fractions

  • For equal denominators: \[ \frac{a}{d}\pm\frac{b}{d}=\frac{a\pm b}{d}. \]
  • For different denominators:
    1. Find the least common denominator (LCD = LCM of denominators).
    2. Convert each fraction to an equivalent one with that LCD.
    3. Add or subtract numerators, keep the common denominator.
    4. Simplify if possible.

Example: $ \frac{5}{24}+\frac{3}{40}=\frac{25}{120}+\frac{9}{120}=\frac{34}{120}=\frac{17}{60}.$

6. Multiplying and Dividing Fractions

  • Multiplication: multiply numerators and denominators directly. \[ \frac{a}{b}√ó\frac{c}{d}=\frac{a¬∑c}{b¬∑d}. \]
  • Division: multiply by the reciprocal. \[ \frac{a}{b}√∑\frac{c}{d}=\frac{a}{b}√ó\frac{d}{c}=\frac{a¬∑d}{b¬∑c}. \]
  • Reciprocal: for non-zero $a$, $a^{-1}=\frac1a$.

7. Decimals and Place Value

Every digit‚Äôs position gives its value. To the right of the decimal point: tenths $(10^{-1})$, hundredths $(10^{-2})$, thousandths $(10^{-3})$, etc.

Example: $37.482=3√ó10^1+7√ó10^0+4√ó10^{-1}+8√ó10^{-2}+2√ó10^{-3}.$

8. Converting Fractions ‚Üî Decimals

  • Fraction ‚Üí Decimal: divide numerator by denominator.
  • Decimal ‚Üí Fraction: write the digits after the decimal as the numerator and the corresponding power of 10 as the denominator, then simplify.

Examples: $\frac{3}{4}=0.75,$ $0.625=\frac{625}{1000}=\frac58.$

9. Repeating Decimals as Fractions

Let $x$ represent the repeating decimal. Move the decimal point so that the repeated block aligns, subtract to eliminate it, and solve for $x$.

Example: $x=0.\overline{36}$ $100x=36.\overline{36}$ Subtract ‚Üí $99x=36$ ‚Üí $x=\frac{36}{99}=\frac49.$

10. Percent, Ratios and Proportions

  • Percent means ‚Äúper hundred‚Äù: $76\%=\frac{76}{100}=0.76$.
  • Ratio: comparison of two quantities $a$ and $b$, written $a:b$ or $\frac{a}{b}$.
  • Proportion: statement that two ratios are equal, \[ \frac{a}{b}=\frac{c}{d}. \] Cross-products are equal: $ad=bc$.

  • Percent increase: \[ \frac{\text{New ‚Äì Old}}{\text{Old}}√ó100\%. \]

Exponents and Radicals

1. Meaning of an Exponent

An exponent tells how many times to multiply the base by itself. \[ x^n=\underbrace{x√óx√ó\cdots√óx}_{n\text{ times}}. \] Example: $2^5=2√ó2√ó2√ó2√ó2=32.$ The base $x$ may be any real number; $n$ is called the power or exponent.

2. Basic Exponent Rules

  • Product Rule: $x^a¬∑x^b=x^{a+b}$ (same base ‚Üí add exponents).
  • Power Rule: $(x^a)^b=x^{ab}$ (power of a power ‚Üí multiply exponents).
  • Power of a Product: $(xy)^a=x^a y^a$.
  • Power of a Quotient: $\left(\dfrac{x}{y}\right)^a=\dfrac{x^a}{y^a}$ ($y\neq0$).
  • Quotient Rule: $\dfrac{x^a}{x^b}=x^{a-b}$ (same base ‚Üí subtract exponents).
  • Zero Exponent: $x^0=1$ (for $x\neq0$).
  • Negative Exponent: $x^{-n}=\dfrac{1}{x^n}$.

3. Special Cases and Observations

  • $0^n=0$ for $n>0$.
  • $1^n=1$ for all $n$.
  • Exponents distribute only over multiplication or division, not addition: $(a+b)^2\ne a^2+b^2$; instead $(a+b)^2=a^2+2ab+b^2$.

4. Simplifying Exponential Expressions

Apply the laws systematically, combine like bases, and re-write with positive exponents. Example: \[ \dfrac{2^5x^3y^{-2}}{4x^{-1}y^4} =\dfrac{2^5}{2^2}\;x^{3-(-1)}\;y^{-2-4} =2^3x^4y^{-6} =\dfrac{8x^4}{y^6}. \]

5. Roots and Radical Notation

A root is the inverse of an exponent. The symbol $\sqrt[n]{\,}$ is the radical sign, $n$ is the index, and the value inside is the radicand.

  • Square root: $(\sqrt{x})^2=x$ for $x‚â•0$.
  • Cube root: $(\sqrt[3]{x})^3=x$ ‚Äì may be negative or positive.
  • Fourth root: $(\sqrt[4]{x})^4=x$ (principal root non-negative).

6. Radical ‚Äì Exponent Relationship

Roots can be written as fractional exponents: \[ \sqrt[n]{x}=x^{1/n},\qquad \sqrt[n]{x^m}=x^{m/n}. \] Example: $\sqrt[3]{x^4}=x^{4/3}.$

7. Operations with Radicals

  • Product Rule: $\sqrt[n]{a}\sqrt[n]{b}=\sqrt[n]{ab}$ for $a,b‚â•0$.
  • Quotient Rule: $\dfrac{\sqrt[n]{a}}{\sqrt[n]{b}}=\sqrt[n]{\dfrac{a}{b}}$ ($b>0$).
  • Cannot combine radicals with different indices or different radicands.

8. Simplifying Square Roots

Break the radicand into perfect square and remainder factors: \[ \sqrt{72}=\sqrt{36√ó2}=\sqrt{36}\sqrt{2}=6\sqrt{2}. \] Factor out any perfect squares; the remaining radicand must have no square factor other than 1.

9. Rationalizing Denominators

A radical expression is in simplified form when no radical remains in the denominator. Multiply by a form of 1 that eliminates the root:

  • For a single radical: $\displaystyle \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}=\frac{\sqrt{5}}{5}$.
  • For a binomial denominator (conjugate method): \[ \frac{1}{3+\sqrt{2}}¬∑\frac{3-\sqrt{2}}{3-\sqrt{2}} =\frac{3-\sqrt{2}}{9-2}=\frac{3-\sqrt{2}}{7}. \]

10. Estimating Square Roots

Locate two perfect squares between which the number lies and interpolate. Example: $\sqrt{50}$ lies between $\sqrt{49}=7$ and $\sqrt{64}=8$, so ‚âà 7.1.

11. Powers and Roots Together

When both appear, use exponent form and apply the laws of exponents carefully: \[ \sqrt[3]{x^5}=x^{5/3},\qquad (\sqrt{x})^3=x^{3/2}. \]

12. Scientific Notation and Powers of 10

Scientific notation uses exponents to represent very large or small numbers: \[ a√ó10^n,\qquad 1‚â§a<10. \] To convert:

  • Move the decimal after the first non-zero digit.
  • Count how many places it moved ‚Üí that‚Äôs $n$.
  • Left ‚Üí positive $n$; right ‚Üí negative $n$.

Example: $53\,000=5.3√ó10^4,$ $0.00042=4.2√ó10^{-4}.$

  • Multiplication: \[ (a√ó10^m)(b√ó10^n)=(a b)√ó10^{m+n}. \]
  • Division: \[ \frac{a√ó10^m}{b√ó10^n}=\frac{a}{b}√ó10^{m-n}. \]

Example: \[ \dfrac{(2.3√ó10^{-4})(6.4√ó10^{12})}{4.2√ó10^{10}} =3.5√ó10^{-2}. \]

Measurement and Conversions

1. Concept of Measurement

A measurement compares a quantity with a standard unit. All measurements have two parts:

  • The number ‚Äì how many of the unit.
